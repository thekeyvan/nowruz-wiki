# The Fire & The Prep: Welcoming the New Year

Nowruz is much more than a single moment of celebration at the equinox; it is an entire season of intense preparation, both physical and spiritual. Before the new year can begin, the old year—with all its dust, darkness, and bad luck—must be completely swept away.

## Khaneh Tekani: Shaking the House

Weeks before the new year arrives, households across Iran and neighboring countries engage in a ritual of massive spring cleaning known as *Khaneh Tekani*. The literal translation of this phrase is **"shaking the house."** 

This is not a casual dusting. Every rug is hauled outside and beaten, every curtain is washed, windows are scrubbed until they shine, and every forgotten corner of the home is meticulously cleaned. The philosophy behind this is simple but profound: you cannot welcome the freshness, purity, and bright energy of spring into a home that is still clinging to the dust and stagnation of the past year. 

By cleaning the physical space, families are also conceptually cleansing their lives, preparing a blank canvas for the new year.

## Chaharshanbe Suri: The Festival of Fire

As the physical house is cleansed, the spiritual house must also be purified. This culminates on the eve of the last Wednesday before Nowruz in a loud, chaotic, and beautiful festival known as *Chaharshanbe Suri* (The Festival of Fire).

As the sun sets, the streets, alleyways, and public parks come alive with bonfires. In a tradition that dates back thousands of years to ancient Zoroastrian fire veneration, people of all ages line up to literally leap over the roaring flames.

As they jump through the fire, they chant a traditional rhyme:

> *"Zardi-ye man az toh, sorkhi-ye toh az man!"*
> **"My yellow to you, your red to me!"**

In this chant, "yellow" represents the pallor of sickness, weakness, and the bad luck accumulated over the past year. "Red" represents the warmth, health, vitality, and fiery energy of the flames. By leaping over the fire, the jumper gives the flames their hardships and takes back the fire's strength.

### Qashogh Zani (Spoon Banging)

Alongside the bonfires, another tradition takes place that bears a striking resemblance to Halloween trick-or-treating. In a ritual called *Qashogh Zani* (spoon banging), children (and often playful adults) drape themselves in long veils or chadors to hide their identities. They roam the neighborhood paths, banging spoons loudly against metal bowls or pots to ward off unlucky spirits, stopping at doors to receive nuts, chocolates, or cash from their neighbors.

Through water, soap, and fire, the slate is wiped clean. The people are now ready to welcome the exact moment of the new year.
