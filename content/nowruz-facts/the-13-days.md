# The 13 Days of Joy

Nowruz is a marathon of celebration. While the exact moment of the equinox is marked by families gathering privately around the Haft-Seen table, the festival itself stretches out for exactly 13 days of socializing, feasting, and joy.

## The Traditions of the First Twelve Days

During the first 12 days, the country essentially comes to a halt. Schools are closed, and businesses operate on holiday hours. The time is dedicated strictly to *Eid Didani*—visiting family and friends.

There is a strict, unwritten protocol to these visits: the younger generation must first visit the eldest members of the family (grandparents and great-aunts/uncles), followed by older siblings, and eventually friends. 

At every stop, guests are greeted with vast quantities of tea, pastries (*shirini*), bowls of mixed nuts (*ajeel*), and fresh fruit. For the children, the visits are particularly lucrative: elders distribute *Eidi*, which is traditionally crisp, newly minted banknotes, often kept tucked inside the pages of the Quran or the Divan of Hafez to bless the money before giving it.

## Haji Firooz: The Herald of Spring

Roam the streets during this time in Iran, and you will likely encounter *Haji Firooz*. He is a traditional folklore character, dressed in a bright red outfit with a face covered in soot. 

Armed with a tambourine, Haji Firooz dances through the streets, singing rhythmic, joyful, and often comical songs to announce the arrival of spring and demand (in good fun) loose change from passersby. His black face and red clothes are thought by some historians to be a representation of the ancient Sumerian deity of agriculture returning from the dark underworld into the bright spring.

## Day 13: Sizdah Bedar (Getting Rid of 13)

In Persian culture, the number 13 is historically considered unlucky. To avoid the bad luck that might befall a household on the 13th day of the new year, an ingenious solution was created: no one stays home!

On the 13th day, known as *Sizdah Bedar* (which roughly translates to "getting rid of the 13th"), entire cities empty out. Millions of people pack overflowing picnic baskets, grab their portable grills, and head out into nature. Every park, forest, mountain base, and patch of grass is blanketed with families. It is a massive, nationwide tailgate party celebrating nature. Men play volleyball, women chat over endless cups of tea, and the smell of grilling kebabs fills the air.

### The Release of the Sabzeh

*Sizdah Bedar* is also the day the Haft-Seen table is finally dismantled. The *Sabzeh*—the lush green wheatgrass sprouts that have been sitting on the table for 13 days—is brought along to the picnic.

It is believed that over the 13 days, the *Sabzeh* has absorbed all the sickness, arguments, and bad luck that might have plagued the family in the coming year. At the end of the picnic, families find a running river or stream and toss the *Sabzeh* into the water. As the green shoots float away, they carry the family's misfortune with them, ensuring that the rest of the year will be filled with health, happiness, and prosperity.
