# The Ancient Roots of Nowruz

Nowruz is not just a date on a calendar; it is a profound celebration of human existence tied directly to the rhythms of the cosmos. Translating to "New Day" in Persian, it occurs at the exact astronomical moment of the vernal equinox—the precise second when the sun crosses the celestial equator and day and night are of perfectly equal length.

## The Zoroastrian Heritage

Before the arrival of Islam in the 7th century, the Persian Empire was predominantly Zoroastrian. Zoroastrianism, one of the world's oldest monotheistic religions, was built upon a dualistic cosmology: the eternal struggle between good and evil, light and darkness, truth and deceit. 

In this ancient faith, Nowruz was the holiest day of the year. It symbolized the ultimate triumph of good (represented by light, warmth, and spring) over evil (represented by darkness, cold, and winter). 

**The Spirit of Summer**
A beautiful concept in Zoroastrianism is the spirit of *Rapithwin*. Rapithwin is the personification of noon and summer. According to tradition, when the cold, dark winter descends, Rapithwin retreats deep beneath the earth to keep the roots of the plants warm and alive. On the day of Nowruz, as spring bursts forth, Rapithwin emerges above ground once more, bringing warmth back to the world.

## The Myth of King Jamshid

While astronomy and religion play their part, Persian mythology points to a more magical origin. The *Shahnameh* (The Book of Kings), the epic poem of Greater Iran, credits the legendary King Jamshid with the creation of the festival. 

The story tells of a time when the world was plunged into a devastating, apocalyptic winter that threatened to wipe out all of mankind. King Jamshid, possessing a glorious jewel-studded throne, commanded demons to raise him into the heavens. He flew through the sky like the sun itself, radiating warmth and light, saving humanity from the frost. 

The day he descended back to earth, bringing with him the rebirth of the world, was declared "Nowruz."

## The Empire's Golden Era

Historical records, beyond myth, show that by 538 BCE, Cyrus the Great—founder of the massive Achaemenid Empire—had officially declared Nowruz a national holiday. 

During this era, the magnificent ceremonies lasted for nearly two months. At the ancient capital of Persepolis, massive limestone bas-reliefs still stand today, depicting dignitaries, nobles, and emissaries from all corners of the vast Persian Empire—from Ethiopia to India—bringing Nowruz tributes and gifts to the *Shahanshah* (King of Kings).

Today, over 300 million people worldwide celebrate Nowruz, maintaining a 3,000-year unbroken chain of joy, renewal, and hope.
