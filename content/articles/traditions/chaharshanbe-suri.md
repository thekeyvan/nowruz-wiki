# Chaharshanbe Suri

**Chaharshanbe Suri** (Persian: چهارشنبه‌سوری), often translated as the "Festival of Fire," is an Iranian festival celebrated on the eve of the last Wednesday before Nowruz (the Iranian New Year). It is a prelude to the new year and acts as a purification ritual.

## Core Traditions

### Jumping Over the Fire
The primary and most famous ritual of Chaharshanbe Suri takes place after sunset. People gather in streets, backyards, and public squares to light bonfires. Participants then take turns jumping over the flames.

As they leap over the fire, they chant the traditional phrase:
> *"Zardi-ye man az toh, sorkhi-ye toh az man."*

**Translation:** "My yellowness to you, your redness to me." 
**Meaning:** The chant is a symbolic exchange with the fire. The "yellowness" represents sickness, pallor, and the problems of the old year. The "redness" represents warmth, health, energy, and the vitality of the fire, which the jumper hopes to take into the new year.

### Qashogh Zani (Spoon-Banging)
Similar to the Western tradition of Halloween trick-or-treating, *Qashogh Zani* involves people (mostly children and young adults) disguising themselves, often wrapping themselves in a chador or dark cloak. They walk through the neighborhood banging spoons against metal bowls to make noise, which is traditionally said to ward off evil spirits. They knock on neighbors' doors and receive sweets, nuts, or money in their bowls.

### Kuzeh Shekani (Smashing the Jug)
An older tradition involves smashing a clay jug (*kuzeh*). Historically, water was stored in clay jugs, which would harbor bacteria over the year. Smashing the old jug and replacing it with a new one for the new year was a practice rooted in hygiene, which evolved into a ritual of transferring bad luck into the jug and destroying it. 

## Historical Context
Chaharshanbe Suri is deeply rooted in ancient Zoroastrian traditions, where fire is considered a sacred element representing purity, truth, and the divine presence. The festival serves as a way to honor the dead, purify oneself, and drive away evil spirits before the holy days of the new year begin. 

Despite various historical attempts by different governments to ban or suppress the festival due to safety concerns or religious reasons, Chaharshanbe Suri remains widely popular and is celebrated fiercely by Iranians worldwide.
