# Haft-Sin

The **Haft-Sin** (Persian: هفت‌سین, meaning "Seven S's") is a traditional table setting assembled during the Nowruz holiday. It is the visual and symbolic centerpiece of the Iranian New Year celebration. 

Families gather around the Haft-Sin table during *Tahvil-e Saal*, the exact moment of the vernal equinox, to exchange wishes for the coming year. The table remains on display in the home until the 13th day of the new year (*Sizdah Bedar*), when the sprouted greens are finally returned to nature.

## The Core Seven Items
The tradition requires the placement of seven distinct items, each of which must begin with the Persian letter "Seen" (س). Each item represents a core hope, value, or natural element for the new year:

### 1. Sabzeh (Sprouts)
* **Symbolizes:** Rebirth, renewal, and the connection between human life and nature.
* **Details:** *Sabzeh* consists of wheat, barley, mung bean, or lentil sprouts grown in a dish. Families begin growing the seeds a couple of weeks before Nowruz so they are lush and green for the new year. The sprouts are usually tied with a red ribbon to symbolize the cycle of life. On the 13th day of the new year, it is cast into running water to carry away bad luck.

### 2. Samanu (Sweet Wheat Pudding)
* **Symbolizes:** Power, strength, affluence, and the sweetness of life.
* **Details:** *Samanu* is a sweet, rich paste made entirely from germinated wheat and water, with no added sugar. The cooking process is notoriously difficult and time-consuming, often taking days and involving a communal effort where neighbors and family members take turns stirring a massive copper pot. 

### 3. Senjed (Dried Oleaster)
* **Symbolizes:** Love, affection, and wisdom.
* **Details:** *Senjed* is the dried fruit of the oleaster (or wild olive) tree. In ancient Persian medicine and folklore, the scent of the oleaster tree's blossoms was believed to inspire love and affection, making it a symbol of strengthening emotional bonds in the coming year.

### 4. Seer (Garlic)
* **Symbolizes:** Medicine, health, and protection.
* **Details:** Garlic is deeply respected in traditional medicine for its healing properties. Placed on the Haft-Sin table, it represents a wish for physical well-being in the new year and acts as a ward against illness and evil spirits.

### 5. Seeb (Apple)
* **Symbolizes:** Beauty, vitality, and health.
* **Details:** A vibrant, usually red, apple is placed on the table to represent natural beauty and a healthy body. In some traditions, the apple is placed in a bowl of water, where its spinning represents the turning of the earth.

### 6. Somagh (Sumac)
* **Symbolizes:** The color of the sunrise, the triumph of light over darkness, and the "spice" of life.
* **Details:** This crushed, deep-red berry powder is a staple in Iranian cuisine. On the Haft-Sin table, its vibrant color represents the dawn of a new day and the victory of good (light) over evil (darkness).

### 7. Serkeh (Vinegar)
* **Symbolizes:** Age, patience, and the wisdom that comes with time.
* **Details:** Because vinegar takes a long time to properly ferment and age, it represents the patience required to endure life's hardships and the wisdom gained through experience. It is also associated with cleanliness and preservation.

## Additional and Optional Elements
While the seven "S" items are mandatory for a traditional table, families typically adorn the space with other deeply symbolic objects to complete the display:

* **A Mirror (Ayeneh):** Usually placed at the top or back of the table. It represents self-reflection, truth, and the boundless sky.
* **Lit Candles (Sham'):** Placed in front of the mirror, representing enlightenment, happiness, and the sacred element of fire in Zoroastrianism. It is customary to light one candle for each child in the household.
* **Goldfish (Mahi Ghermez):** Swimming in a glass bowl, the fish represent life, movement, and the astrological sign of Pisces (Esfand), which is ending as the new year of Aries (Farvardin) begins.
* **Painted Eggs (Tokhm-e Morgh-e Rangi):** Symbolize fertility, creation, and human origins. Family members, especially children, often paint the eggs themselves. There is usually one egg for each member of the family.
* **Coins (Sekkeh):** Represent a wish for wealth, prosperity, and financial success in the coming year.
* **A Holy or Poetry Book:** Families often place a text of spiritual or cultural importance on the table. This could be the Quran, the Avesta (the Zoroastrian holy text), the *Shahnameh* (Book of Kings), or the *Divan of Hafez*. After the new year begins, it is common to randomly open the poetry of Hafez for a *Faal* (fortune or guidance for the year).
* **Hyacinth Flowers (Sonbol):** Known for their beautiful fragrance, these spring flowers bring the fresh scent of the new season into the home.
* **A Clock:** Represents the passing of time and the anticipation of the exact moment of the equinox.
* **Traditional Pastries and Nuts (Ajil):** Sweets like baklava, *noghl* (sugar-coated almonds), and *shirini nokhodchi* (chickpea cookies) are placed on the table to ensure a sweet year, alongside mixed nuts to offer to visiting guests.

## Historical Origins
The exact historical origins of the Haft-Sin are debated by scholars. Some historians suggest it evolved from a pre-Islamic Zoroastrian tradition called *Haft-Chin* (Seven Crops), where seven different crops were placed on a table as an agricultural offering. Over time, particularly after the Islamic conquest of Persia and the linguistic shift, the items were adapted to fit the Arabic/Persian alphabet, solidifying into the modern "Haft-Sin."
