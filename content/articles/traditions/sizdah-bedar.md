# Sizdah Bedar

**Sizdah Bedar** (Persian: سیزده‌بدر), also known as **Nature Day** (روز طبیعت), is an Iranian festival held annually on the thirteenth day of the first month (Farvardin) of the Iranian calendar. It marks the official end of the Nowruz holidays. 

The name translates roughly to "getting rid of the thirteenth" or "thirteenth outdoors." In Persian culture, as in many others, the number 13 is traditionally considered unlucky. To avoid the bad luck associated with the 13th day of the new year, people leave their homes and spend the entire day outdoors in nature.

## Customs and Activities

### Picnicking in Nature
On Sizdah Bedar, parks, forests, riverbanks, and countryside areas are filled with families and friends. It is customary to pack large, elaborate picnics, play outdoor games, listen to music, and dance. Staying indoors on this day is considered an invitation for bad luck for the rest of the year.

### Releasing the Sabzeh
The most important ritual of the day involves the *Sabzeh* (the sprouted wheatgrass or lentils) that was grown for the Haft-Sin table. The Sabzeh is believed to have absorbed all the negative energy, sickness, and bad luck from the household over the previous twelve days. 

On the 13th day, families take their Sabzeh with them to nature and throw it into a body of flowing water (like a river or stream). This act symbolizes washing away the bad luck and returning the plant to nature.

### Tying the Grass (Gereh Zadan-e Sabzeh)
Before throwing the Sabzeh into the water, it is a common tradition for single people—particularly young women—to tie the blades of grass into knots. 

While tying the knot, they make a wish, usually related to finding a partner, getting married, or having a child before the next Nowruz. The untying of the knot by the water's current is believed to symbolize the opening of their fortunes and the granting of their wish.

## Culinary Traditions
Food is a massive component of Sizdah Bedar. Families typically eat the leftovers of traditional Nowruz meals or cook outdoors. The most iconic dish associated with this day is **Ash-e Reshteh**, a thick and hearty noodle soup made with herbs, beans, and kashk (fermented whey). Eating noodles is believed to symbolize the untangling of life's difficulties.
